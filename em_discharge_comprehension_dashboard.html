Overview

Patients discharged from the emergency department frequently leave without a clear understanding of their diagnosis, medications, or return precautions — a well-documented contributor to preventable 72-hour return visits, medication errors, and adverse outcomes. Despite this, the specific domains of comprehension failure remain inconsistently characterised across diverse urban ED populations. This study proposes a prospective cross-sectional survey of ED patients to quantify comprehension of key discharge instruction elements and identify the patient-level and system-level factors most strongly associated with poor understanding, with the ultimate aim of informing a targeted educational intervention.

Why This Research Matters Now
~80%
Patients Forget or Misunderstand
Studies consistently show that up to 80% of patients forget or misunderstand at least one element of their discharge instructions within 30 minutes of receiving them.
36%
US Adults Have Low Health Literacy
Over a third of American adults read below a 6th-grade level, yet most discharge paperwork is written at a 10th-grade level or higher. The gap is widest in urban EDs.
$1,500+
Average Cost Per Return Visit
Preventable 72-hour return visits cost the healthcare system billions annually. Poor discharge comprehension is one of the most modifiable contributing factors.
Research Questions
Proposed Questions — Select One Primary Focus
Primary Recommendation

"What proportion of patients discharged from the Emergency Department can correctly identify their primary diagnosis, prescribed medications, return precautions, and follow-up instructions — and which of these domains shows the lowest comprehension rate?"

Alternative A · Predictors Focus

"What patient-level factors — age, primary language, health literacy score, time of day, wait time — are independently associated with poor discharge instruction comprehension in an urban ED?"

Alternative B · Intervention Focus

"Does a teach-back or visual-aid discharge intervention improve patient comprehension of diagnosis, medications, and return precautions compared to standard printed instructions alone?"

Proposed Survey Instrument
Core Questions — Administered 20–30 Minutes After Discharge Papers Given
1
What did the doctor say is wrong with you today?
Open-ended. Coded as correct / partially correct / incorrect against chart diagnosis. Primary outcome measure.
Diagnosis
2
What new medications were you prescribed today, and what are they for?
Open-ended. Coded against discharge prescription list. Captures both recall and understanding of indication.
Medications
3
What symptoms should cause you to come back to the ER?
Open-ended. Coded against documented return precautions. Identifies highest-risk knowledge gap.
Return Precautions
4
Who are you supposed to follow up with, and when?
Open-ended. Coded against discharge follow-up instructions. Captures both provider type and timeframe.
Follow-Up
5
How clearly did the doctor or nurse explain your discharge instructions? (1–5 scale)
Likert scale. Allows correlation between perceived clarity and actual comprehension — these often diverge.
Perception
6
What is the highest level of education you completed? What is your primary language?
Demographic covariates. Enables subgroup analysis by literacy proxy and language concordance with provider.
Demographics

Gaps in Current Literature
Most published studies are over a decade old — post-COVID ED volumes, staffing pressures, and digital discharge systems have changed the landscape
Existing literature rarely identifies which specific instruction domain fails most — diagnosis, medications, return precautions, or follow-up
Almost no data from urban safety-net hospitals with high proportions of non-English-speaking and low-literacy patients — exactly the population at highest risk
The gap between patient-perceived clarity and actual comprehension is understudied — patients often rate instructions as clear while demonstrating poor recall
No direct link established between measured comprehension scores and 72-hour return visit rates at the individual patient level
Studies rarely examine time-of-day or shift-level variation — overnight discharges may have systematically worse comprehension due to staffing and patient fatigue
